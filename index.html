A love story

Imagine wearing a winter coat in Caribbean heat?

The marine worm Laxus oneistus, does just that!

This tiny marine creature has a permanent symbiont bacterial "coat": Candidatus Thiosymbion oneisti, clinging to its surface - creating an everlasting relationship.

Discover below unique features of this oftenly unnoticed alliance, to fall a bit more in love with our amazing planet!

Extraordinary features!

Marine sand protagonists!

  • Globally distributed in shallow-water marine sediments.
  • Abundant in the Caribbean Sea.
  • Fun fact: The nematode Laxus oneistus was discovered in the Carrie Bow Cay field station in Belize! (Ott et al., 1995)

One of a kind symbiosis!

  • In nature, most symbionts reside inside their hosts (endosymbiosis).
  • On the other hand, ectosymbiosis or "living in the surface", is extremely rare.
  • What makes this symbiosis even rarer? With one or two exceptions, animals associate with multiple and different microbal species, but L. oneistus is very selective and carries only Candidatus Thiosymbion oneisti!

The bacteria gives a superpower!

- Ever smelled rotten eggs while digging in sea sand? That is a form sulfur in action, and it is abundant in marine sand! While harmless to us, it is toxic for tiny animals! - Good news! Ca. T. oneisti "eats" sulfur compounds to make energy. Thus, scientists hypothesize that the bacteria shields the worm from sulfide poisoning, and now the worm can make use of an environment, otherwise inhospitable. The migration hypothesis (Ott et al) postulates that L. oneistus moves vertically across the sediment, encountering in upper layers: oxygenated sand, and in deeper ones: anoxic-sulfidic sand.
